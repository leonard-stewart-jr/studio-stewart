Architecture has always shaped the world through space and material, but we rarely stop to ask what those choices actually mean. Matter Matters is a research-driven branch of an architecture firm that pushes back against this disconnect. We believe every material holds more than technical value; it carries weight, context, and consequence.

We approach architecture as a process of investigation, not just invention. Our studio works across scales and formats, moving between lab testing, field documentation, and public translation. Whether we are isolating a single material for analysis or building a broader picture through data, we focus on making complex systems legible. Tools like our H.C. Index help structure our research, but they are only one expression of a much larger commitment; to rethink how material choices shape practice, policy, and perception.

THE CRISIS:

MATERIAL EXTRACTION & INVISIBLE LABOR

Within the architecture profession, most conversations around sustainability focus on performance or appearance. We talk about energy savings, recycled content, or carbon footprints. Still, very few people ask what actually happens between the raw material and the final product. Steel beams do not begin in neat stacks, and lithium batteries are not born in plastic packaging. These materials are cooked, crushed, hauled, and transformed; often through destructive or dangerous processes. That story is rarely told, and even less understood.Few in the profession are taught to ask who touches these materials, or under what conditions. Mining, processing, and manufacturing are often outsourced to regions where regulations are weak and labor is cheap. Workers face exposure, injury, and exploitation long before a product ever reaches a job site. These efforts are rarely visible in architectural education, client conversations, or construction documents. That invisibility is not accidental; it is built into the system.We see one part of the problem as misunderstanding, and the other part as disinterest. Clients ask for LEED or green credentials without understanding what makes something truly sustainable. They chase materials that photograph well or carry popular labels, without ever asking where those materials came from or what their production required. Architecture has become too comfortable with distance; distance from origin, labor, accountability, and consequence. Matter Matters was created to close that gap. We believe that knowing the story of a material, as well as understanding its path, its risk, and its impact, is the first step in the journey toward ethical design.

OUR APPROACH

Matter Matters operates through five main activities:

Research & DiscoveryWe trace the full journey of materials from extraction to installation. This includes mapping supply chains, identifying labor risks, and cataloging ethical alternatives. We also study the political and economic systems that shape material markets, from rare earth mining in the Congo to aluminum production in China.

Material TestingOur studio conducts in-house testing on traditional and emerging materials. These include strength tests, bend tests, degradation studies, and chemical reactivity reports. We document not only technical properties but also the human cost and environmental load of each material.

Ethical Material ConsultingWe work with architecture offices, educators, and public institutions to evaluate the sourcing of their materials. Our services include labor risk assessments, recommendations for safer alternatives, and alignment with ESG, carbon, or transparency goals. The goal is not just compliance, but accountability

Speculative PrototypingWe design experimental construction systems using unconventional, underused, or reclaimed materials. These speculative works challenge what counts as buildable and ask new questions about value; beyond form, structure, or surface. They are built to provoke, not just perform.

Public EducationThrough exhibitions, zines, school workshops, and data-driven artifacts, we make material ethics visible and understandable to wider audiences. One of our most public-facing tools is the H.C. Index, which reimagines the periodic table by classifying common elements according to their labor conditions and ethical impact.

LOOKING AHEADWHAT DOES OUR FUTURE HOLD:

Matter Matters is not interested in becoming a traditional firm. We do not pitch commercial buildings or brand ourselves with sleek visuals. We operate as a lab, a journal, and a platform for material ethics. We imagine a future where architects are accountable to more than budgets, schedules, and appearances.Our vision is simple: REVEAL what has long remained hidden. If materials matter, then so do the people who make them.

FRAMEWORKThis practice engages several major themes from THE practice:Labor: We treat labor as a design parameter. Every part of our platform centers the conditions, rights, and risks of the people behind materials. Labor is not a side effect of production; it is part of the architecture.

Clients & Money: We do not work with clients in the traditional sense. Our speculative model is supported through public grants, academic partnerships, and open source donations. This structure reduces ethical compromise and allows us to stay independent and transparent. This branch of Studio StewArt is 100% non-profit.

Drawing & Modeling Methods: We use graphic communication, modular layouts, and icon systems as tools for critical research. Drawing is not just a way to represent a project; it is a way to uncover systems, provoke questions, and share knowledge with others.

Relationship to the Public: We do not see the public as passive observers. The people we design for are also the people we learn from. Our tools are built to be used by anyone, regardless of whether they are architects.

THE H.C. INDEX HUMAN CAPITAL PERIODIC TABLE

Human Capital Index Periodic Ethics of The Element TableThe H.C. Index is a research driven artifact that reimagines the periodic table through the lens of labor. Each element is color coded by human capital risk: red for severe, yellow for medium, green for low, and gray for inactive. These categories reflect one year of sourcing analysis; the data combines trade records, environmental studies, and human rights investigations.

At the bottom of the table, six of the most widely used material systems in architecture appear as icons: solar panels, steel, glass, plastic, concrete, and paint. In the interactive version, each icon toggles a state; when selected, it grows slightly and shifts from black and white to color, triggering a visual highlight across all related elements above.

The Index supports both digital and printed formats. In the analog zine version, each icon includes a brief fact or callout. In the digital version, each element opens a modal window that reveals additional context: who is involved in its production, where it moves, and what the human cost looks like.

This artifact is not static, and it is not complete. It is a living tool; a prompt to reveal hidden systems and encourage new forms of accountability. The table shifts from neutral chart to ethical framework; a way to read labor as clearly as we read performance
