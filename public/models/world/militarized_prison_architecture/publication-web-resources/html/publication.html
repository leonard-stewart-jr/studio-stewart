WHY IT MATTERS

By the mid-nineteenth century, the idea that architecture could discipline had become a global strategy. Governments across Europe and the Americas used military-style planning to formalize incarceration, turning prisons into extensions of the state. With radial towers, regimented corridors, and rigid separation, these buildings reshaped confinement into a mechanical system of control, order, and obedience. The Panopticon was no longer a theory; it had been absorbed into

national infrastructure.

Following the rise of Enlightenment prison theory, the nineteenth century marked a turning point where state power became mapped directly onto prison floorplans. Across Western Europe and the Americas, militarized designs imposed discipline through geometry, creating architectural systems that could divide, watch, and correct with near-total efficiency. Unlike earlier carceral forms that emphasized fear or repentance, these buildings were built to manage movement, isolate the body, and train behavior without direct contact.

WORLD

Elsewhere, earlier institutions like Maison de Force were retrofitted with militarized zoning, while new facilities in Mexico City and St. Petersburg integrated radial surveillance into fortress-like structures. Central plumbing, heating, and mechanical guards became tools for managing large inmate populations without direct confrontation. These plans were not just functional—they were ideological, embedding the state’s authority into the walls, thresholds, and corridors of every structure. By the 1880s, carceral design had become a form of government.

Pentonville Prison in London and Moabit Prison in Berlin introduced radial layouts with long, symmetrical corridors branching from central hubs, allowing minimal staff to supervise maximum inmates. Silence and labor were enforced through repetition and separation. Prisión Modelo in Madrid added symbolic and religious order, placing the chapel at the core of a radiating layout that merged faith with inspection. In Pittsburgh, the First Western Penitentiary pushed isolation further, embedding solitary confinement within tight, cell-lined rows. Across these examples, movement was choreographed, and visibility was maximized.
