WHY IT MATTERS

The Nazi concentration camps stand as the most brutal reminder of what happens when incarceration is removed from legal oversight and turned into a system of mass extermination. These were not correctional institutions; they were machines of racial terror and political cleansing, architected to erase life. Their layout, labor infrastructure, and mechanized killing procedures left behind more than ruins; they redefined what imprisonment could become under totalitarian rule and forced the world to reconsider the ethics of confinement itself.

By war’s end in 1945, over eighteen million people had passed through the camps, and more than six million were killed. The spatial strategies of these institutions; surveillance, separation, psychological torment, and mass execution, left a permanent scar on global consciousness. They also shaped the postwar human rights framework, influencing the Geneva Conventions, the creation of the United Nations, and a new skepticism toward carceral authority. These were not failed prisons; they were perfectly functioning horrors built on the misuse of state power.

WORLD

Shortly after Adolf Hitler rose to power in 1933, the Nazi regime began building a carceral system meant to suppress dissent and purify society. Dachau, the first camp, opened just weeks into Hitler’s rule and was used to detain political opponents; by 1938, the system expanded to include Jews, Roma, homosexuals, the disabled, Soviet prisoners of war, and anyone deemed an enemy of the Nazi state. These were not prisons with sentences or trials, but spaces of unending degradation.

By the early 1940s, the camp system evolved into a hierarchy: transit camps for sorting, labor camps for extraction, and extermination camps for industrialized murder. Auschwitz, the largest and most infamous, fused all three functions. Its layout included electrified fences, gas chambers disguised as showers, barracks that housed thousands in starvation conditions, and crematoria that ran continuously. The architecture was methodical; every corridor, gate, and train track was part of a larger logistics of genocide.
